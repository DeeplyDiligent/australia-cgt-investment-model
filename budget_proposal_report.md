# Australia investment outlook under the proposed 2026 Budget negative gearing and CGT changes

## Bottom line

If the announced Budget changes become law broadly as described, the future is **less favourable for leveraged established residential property**, **more favourable for genuinely supply-adding new builds**, and **more competitive for shares relative to established property**.

That does **not** mean property becomes a bad asset class. It means the tax system would stop heavily rewarding the classic strategy of buying a low-yield established property, running tax losses against salary, and relying on capital growth plus a 50% CGT discount at exit.

## What was announced

The official Budget explainer says that from **1 July 2027** the Government will:

1. limit negative gearing for residential property investments to **new builds**
2. replace the **50% CGT discount** for individuals, trusts and partnerships with **CPI cost-base indexation**
3. impose a **30% minimum tax on real capital gains**

The explainer also says:

1. properties held before **7:30pm AEST on 12 May 2026** are grandfathered for negative gearing
2. established properties bought after the announcement can still be negatively geared until **30 June 2027**, but not after
3. gains accrued before **1 July 2027** stay under the old CGT rules, with gains after that date moving to the new rules
4. investors in **new builds** can still negative gear and can choose either the old **50% CGT discount** or the new **indexation + minimum-tax** regime on sale

## The most important future implication

The proposal would create a much sharper split between:

1. **established residential property**, which loses most of its tax shelter for new investors
2. **new builds that genuinely add supply**, which keep the most valuable tax support
3. **shares**, which also lose the 50% discount but do not depend on quarantined rental losses, stamp duty, or leverage to the same extent

In other words, the proposal is not just a housing policy. It changes the **ranking of investment strategies**.

## What this means for property versus shares

### Established property versus shares

If you are comparing **established residential property** with **shares / ETFs**, the proposal generally shifts the relative case **toward shares** unless the property has one or more of the following:

1. stronger starting yield
2. unusually strong owner-occupier demand
3. genuine scarcity
4. redevelopment or supply-creation optionality
5. a compelling non-tax reason to believe capital growth will materially exceed shares

Why?

1. both shares and property lose the old 50% CGT framework for gains after 1 July 2027
2. shares still keep liquidity, diversification, lower transaction costs and no stamp duty
3. established property loses the ability to offset losses against salary after the transition period
4. established property still carries concentration risk, maintenance risk and borrowing risk

So in a future shaped by the proposal, **shares become the cleaner default option** and **established property has to earn its place on fundamentals, not tax treatment**.

### New build property versus shares

The proposal is much friendlier to **new builds that genuinely add supply**.

Those assets would still have:

1. negative gearing
2. a better exit-tax choice
3. policy support aligned with the Government's supply objective

That means the most credible property thesis going forward may become:

**new-supply residential with solid economics**

rather than

**established negatively geared residential held mainly for tax and capital growth**

## Should you focus on higher yield or capital growth if negative gearing is effectively gone for established properties?

For **established residential property**, the proposal makes **yield more important** than it is today.

That does not mean capital growth stops mattering. It means the hurdle rate for a low-yield property becomes much higher because:

1. you can no longer use losses to reduce salary and wage tax after the transition period
2. cashflow pain is no longer cushioned in the same way
3. the old 50% CGT discount is gone on post-1 July 2027 gains

### Practical read

1. **High-yield established property** becomes relatively more attractive than low-yield established property.
2. **Scarce established property with owner-occupier depth** can still work, but only if the growth case is genuinely strong.
3. **Low-yield established property bought mainly for tax loss harvesting** becomes much less compelling.
4. **New builds** can still justify a lower yield more easily than established stock because they retain better tax support.

So if the proposal becomes law, the answer is usually:

**for established property, lean more toward yield and cashflow resilience**

not because growth stops mattering, but because **you can no longer rely on tax settings to carry a weak cashflow asset**.

## Likely long-run winners and losers by suburb or asset type

### Likely winners

1. **Boutique new townhouses, duplexes and low-rise infill projects** in scarce established suburbs  
   These may keep the new-build tax advantages without suffering the oversupply problems of big apartment precincts.

2. **Subdividable blocks and redevelopment sites** that can create net new dwellings  
   The policy framework increases the value of land that can be converted into tax-favoured new supply.

3. **Established family suburbs with strong schools, transport and owner-occupier demand**  
   These are less dependent on investor tax benefits because families and owner-occupiers provide deeper end demand.

4. **Older boutique apartments in premium suburbs**  
   If the building has low strata drag, strong location and limited competing supply, it can still hold up because the asset is supported by amenity and affordability rather than tax subsidy.

5. **Regional service hubs with durable rental demand**  
   Yield matters more under the proposal, so places with sticky rents and real employment anchors may become relatively more attractive.

### Likely losers

1. **Low-yield established investor stock in expensive suburbs**  
   This is the clearest loser because the old formula of running losses against salary and waiting for capital growth is directly targeted.

2. **Generic high-rise apartment precincts dominated by investors**  
   These may still qualify as new builds initially, but oversupply, high body-corporate costs, weak land content and resale competition can swamp the tax advantage.

3. **Any property market that depends on investor tax arbitrage more than occupant demand**  
   Once tax support is reduced, weaker underlying economics get exposed.

4. **Established stock with heavy maintenance needs and poor yield**  
   The carry-forward feature helps, but it does not solve ongoing cashflow drag.

### Important nuance

The proposal probably does **not** make all established property bad and all new property good.

The better framing is:

1. established property now needs stronger **real-world economics**
2. new property keeps better **tax economics**
3. investors still need to avoid **bad product**, especially oversupplied high-rise stock

## Direct answer to your main question

With your starting point of **one owner-occupied property and about $100k in stocks**, the proposal makes the default future case look more like:

1. **keep building diversified liquid assets first**, especially if serviceability, flexibility and risk management matter
2. only add residential property where either
   1. it is a genuinely supply-adding new build with good fundamentals, or
   2. it is established stock with unusually good yield, scarcity or redevelopment upside

The biggest strategy that becomes weaker is:

**buying a low-yield established investment property and relying on negative gearing plus the old CGT discount**

The strategies that become stronger are:

1. **shares / ETFs**
2. **selective new builds**
3. **redevelopment-capable residential land**
4. **higher-yield property rather than pure tax-loss property**

## What I would take away from this as an investor

If these changes are enacted broadly as announced:

1. **tax becomes a much smaller reason to buy established property**
2. **cashflow quality matters more**
3. **new-supply property becomes the policy-favoured lane**
4. **shares become a stronger benchmark that property must beat on fundamentals**
5. **suburbs with real owner-occupier demand and scarce supply still matter more than ever**

## Three additional reforms that change the investment calculus

### 1. Discretionary (family) trust minimum tax from 1 July 2028

The budget includes a separate but closely related reform: a **30% minimum tax on the taxable income of discretionary trusts** from 1 July 2028.

Key design points:

1. The tax is levied on the **trustee**, with beneficiaries receiving non-refundable credits.
2. **Fixed trusts** (unit trusts), complying superannuation funds, charitable trusts, deceased estates and special disability trusts are **excluded**.
3. **Primary production income** and certain income for vulnerable minors are excluded.
4. Crucially, **corporate beneficiaries ("bucket companies") lose their advantage** because trust income will now be taxed at the trust level AND again at the company level — effectively ending that strategy.
5. **Three years of rollover relief** is available from 1 July 2027 for investors who wish to restructure out of a discretionary trust structure.

**Why it matters for investors:** Many Australians hold investment properties or share portfolios through a family trust to distribute income to lower-rate beneficiaries. That advantage is largely eliminated from 2028. Investors in this position need to urgently model whether restructuring into a unit trust, company, superannuation fund or individual name makes sense — noting that restructuring itself may trigger CGT and stamp duty.

### 2. Widely held trusts and super funds are exempt from the negative gearing restriction

The negative gearing quarantine applies to **individual investors** buying established residential properties. Investments held through:

- complying superannuation funds (including SMSFs)
- widely held trusts
- build-to-rent developments
- private investors supporting Government Housing programs

...are explicitly **exempt** from the quarantine.

This means SMSF investors in established residential property retain the ability to offset rental losses against other income within the fund. It creates a structural advantage for superannuation-based property investment over personal-name or family trust ownership of established residential property.

### 3. Pre-1985 assets lose CGT exemption for post-2027 gains

Assets acquired before 20 September 1985 (pre-CGT assets) are currently **fully exempt** from capital gains tax. Under the proposal:

1. Gains accrued up to 30 June 2027 remain exempt.
2. Any gain accruing **from 1 July 2027 onwards** is subject to the new indexation and minimum-tax regime.
3. Taxpayers can use a **market valuation at 1 July 2027** or an ATO apportionment formula to split gains.

This affects holders of long-standing pre-CGT assets — family farms, commercial buildings, business interests held since before 1985. These assets are no longer fully sheltered after 1 July 2027.

### 4. Age Pension and income support recipients are exempt from the 30% CGT minimum tax

Income support recipients, including Age Pension recipients, are **exempt** from the 30% minimum tax on real capital gains. They are still subject to the shift to CPI cost-base indexation (which replaces the 50% discount), but are not subject to the minimum tax floor.

This matters for retirement planning: the minimum tax primarily hits high-income investors who could otherwise time an asset sale into a low-income year. That timing strategy is now less effective for working investors — but retirees on income support retain more flexibility.

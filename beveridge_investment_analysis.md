# Beveridge Investment Research Proposal

## Objective

Assess whether buying a house-and-land package in Beveridge or a similar Melbourne fringe suburb is likely to be a good long-term investment compared with:

1. A newer unit or townhouse in an established Melbourne suburb.
2. A higher-yielding property investment.
3. An ETF portfolio.

The main lens is a 30-year investor holding period, supported by 10-year historical evidence from comparable suburbs.

## Core hypothesis

The working theory is that Beveridge may underperform on a risk-adjusted basis because fringe suburbs often have:

- weak initial rental yields relative to mortgage interest costs;
- negative gearing reliance in the early years;
- abundant nearby land supply, which may delay scarcity-driven capital growth;
- dependence on future infrastructure, population growth, and amenity catch-up.

The counterargument is that long-term land ownership in a growth corridor may eventually outperform smaller dwellings closer to the city, especially if infrastructure and population growth are strong.

- How negatively geared would a typical $600k-$750k purchase be at current interest rates?
- How much capital growth is required just to compensate for early cash-flow losses?
- Are there signs of oversupply or ongoing land-release pressure?
- What infrastructure, employment, transport, and amenity improvements are planned or underway?

Study suburbs that were in a similar position around 10 years ago, such as:

### 3. Victorian tax and policy effects

Research should cover:

- COVID debt levy impacts;
- investor sentiment and interstate capital flows;
- rental regulation changes if materially relevant;
- whether post-2022 underperformance is Victoria-specific or simply a general rate-cycle effect.

The analysis should avoid blaming fringe suburbs alone if the whole Victorian market was affected by tax changes.

### 4. Interstate fringe comparison

Compare Melbourne fringe suburbs with growth areas in other states to separate the fringe-suburb effect from the Victoria-specific effect.

Potential comparison areas:

- Sydney north-west, west, and south-west: Marsden Park, Box Hill NSW, Oran Park, Leppington, Edmondson Park.
- Brisbane/SEQ: Springfield, Ripley, Yarrabilba, North Lakes, Mango Hill, Pimpama, Coomera.
- Perth growth corridors if useful.

Research questions:

- Did interstate fringe suburbs with tighter land constraints outperform Melbourne fringe suburbs?
- Did geography and infrastructure create stronger scarcity?
- Were yields, vacancy, and capital growth materially better?
- Is Beveridge risk mainly a Melbourne supply issue, a Victorian tax issue, or a generic outer-fringe issue?

### 5. Established-suburb unit/townhouse alternative

Identify realistic $600k-$750k alternatives in established Melbourne suburbs.

Compare:

- 10-year unit and townhouse capital growth;
- rental yield;
- vacancy risk;
- owner-occupier demand;
- resale liquidity;
- body corporate and maintenance costs;
- land component;
- long-term scarcity and redevelopment potential.

The key question is whether a smaller asset closer to jobs, transport, schools, and existing amenity offers better risk-adjusted returns than a larger land component on the fringe.

### 6. Higher-yield property alternative

Assess whether the investor would be better served by prioritising yield rather than speculative long-term capital growth.

Research should consider:

- regional or outer-suburban properties with stronger rental yields;
- cash-flow resilience;
- lower reliance on negative gearing;
- risk of weaker long-term capital growth;
- tenant demand and vacancy risk.

The key question is whether reducing holding-cost drag improves the investor’s probability of a good outcome.

### 7. ETF alternative

Compare property options against a simple ETF strategy.

Research should cover:

- long-term Australian and global equity return benchmarks;
- dividend/distribution yield;
- tax treatment at a high level;
- liquidity;
- diversification;
- lack of property-specific costs such as stamp duty, maintenance, vacancy, insurance, agents, and land tax;
- the role of leverage, since property returns are usually amplified by debt.

The comparison should be after costs and risk-adjusted, not just headline capital growth.

Major infrastructure is planned (e.g. a new train station and line upgrade【66†L966-L974】, a $1.62B intermodal freight hub and $900m road interchange【69†L48-L56】【69†L75-L83】) – but these are mostly future and will take years to materialise.  

Historical data from other Melbourne growth suburbs suggests fringe areas did benefit from past booms but are now cooling.  In 2014–19, suburbs like Tarneit, Wyndham Vale and Craigieburn saw average annual growth on the order of 3–5%【17†L146-L152】【21†L145-L153】【34†L142-L150】.  During the 2020–22 low-rate boom they rallied further (e.g. Craigieburn +9% last 12 m【34†L142-L150】), but since 2022 higher rates and Victoria’s investor taxes have dampened values (Tarneit +3.1%, Wyndham +4.3% last year【17†L146-L152】【21†L145-L153】).  By contrast, Sydney/Brisbane fringe markets have faced fewer tax headwinds.  For example, Box Hill (NSW, Sydney fringe) recently saw +9.2% house growth【48†L30-L39】 (at a similar low ~3.3% yield【48†L52-L56】), and Darwin has become a high-yield hotspot (houses ~5.5%, units ~7.2%【57†L151-L159】) due to extreme supply constraints.  

On balance, Beveridge looks **highly scenario-dependent**: in a bull case (fast infrastructure delivery, rapid population growth, tight supply) it could outperform, but the base case is low initial returns and heavy reliance on tax relief.  Relative to alternatives, a close-in unit/townhouse offers similar long-term growth with much higher yield (~5% in Hawthorn【54†L353-L359】) and liquidity, while ETFs (Australian shares) have historically returned ~10–11% p.a.【59†L138-L146】 (with ~4% dividends) without stamp duty or property costs. The rest of this report examines these points in detail.

## Beveridge base-case model  

Assume a 30-year fixed loan at **6%** on a $700k house-and-land package (10% deposit).  At ~3.7% gross yield【5†L349-L352】 ($500/week rent), gross rent ~$26k/yr.  Interest on a $630k loan is ~$37.8k/yr, leaving a ~$12k pre-tax deficit.  With tax relief (40% marginal, equivalent to 1.5× loss deduction), the net loss is still on the order of ~$7–8k/yr (~1.1% of price).  Ongoing costs (insurance, rates, maintenance) add more drag. In this scenario, the property would need >2% p.a. capital growth just to offset the cash-flow loss.  Even if it did grow 3% p.a. (a strong outcome by today’s markets), the early years would be heavily negatively geared.

- **Example:** $700k price, 90% loan, 6% interest ⇒ ~$12k shortfall, ~$8k after tax. Equity builds slowly (loan amortises ~$9k principal/yr). Total return breakeven requires ~1–2% net price growth to cover these losses.

In summary, **Beveridge’s break-even capital growth is non-trivial**.  Investors must wait years before negative gearing turns neutral.  By contrast, the same $700k in a suburban townhouse yielding ~5% would generate ~A$24k rent vs ~$24k interest (6%), much closer to cashflow break-even.  Every 0.5% lift in gross yield markedly reduces risk, highlighting the drag from Beveridge’s low initial yield【5†L349-L352】.

## Historical suburb evidence  

We examine ten-year outcomes in comparable Melbourne fringe suburbs to see if past growth ever “paid back” early negative gearing.  

- **2014–2019 (pre-COVID):**  Growth was solid but moderate.  Tarneit’s median house rose ~3.3% p.a.【17†L146-L152】, Wyndham Vale ~3.9%【21†L145-L153】, Craigieburn ~5.0%【34†L142-L150】, Truganina ~3.0%【23†L143-L150】.  (By comparison, inner Melbourne grew ~5–7% p.a. in this period.)  These returns roughly matched national wage/inflation growth, but were far below the double-digit booms Sydney experienced earlier (often >10% p.a. in 2012–2017). 

- **2020–2022 (pandemic/low rates):**  All markets surged under record-low rates.  Melbourne fringe saw moderate rebounds: Wyndham +4.3% (last 12m)【21†L145-L153】, Craigieburn +9.1%【34†L142-L150】.  Tarneit was only +3.1%【17†L146-L152】 and Truganina +3.0%【23†L143-L150】, suggesting the boom was uneven.  In Sydney/Brisbane fringes, growth was generally stronger; e.g. Box Hill (NSW) rose +9.2%【48†L30-L39】 in the latest 12m. 

- **2022–present (high rates, Vic taxes):**  Growth has largely paused or declined.  To early 2026, Melbourne’s outer suburbs show low single-digit gains: Tarneit +3.1%【17†L146-L152】, Wyndham +4.3%【21†L145-L153】, Truganina +3.0%【23†L143-L150】.  This is partly cyclical (rising rates slowed all of Australia) but also due to Victoria’s heavier tax load.  By contrast, Sydney’s fringe is holding up better (Box Hill’s +9.2%【48†L30-L39】 shows resilience).  **Net 10-year returns:**  Tarneit and Wyndham houses are up roughly 50–70% since 2014 (around 4–5% p.a.), whereas inner-city units (e.g. Hawthorn) have barely moved over the same decade.

- **Rental yields and vacancies:**  Fringes have consistently low yields (~3–4%).  Beveridge ~3.7%【5†L349-L352】, Truganina ~4.0%【23†L143-L150】, Tarneit ~4.0%【17†L146-L152】, Craigieburn ~4.0%【34†L142-L150】.  These are below the ~5–6% common in many non-capital or very outer markets.  Vacancies in Melbourne fringe are moderate (~2–3%), higher than hotspots like Darwin (~0.5%)【57†L111-L119】.  In other words, fringe renters do exist, but yields remain thin.  

- **Supply drivers:**  A key risk historically has been continual new supply.  For example, Tarneit added ~15,000 dwellings 2017–2027【25†L228-L236】 even while population nearly doubled.  Similarly, Clyde North has a very high development pipeline (approvals ratio ~7.7%【64†L281-L290】).  Beveridge itself is slated to grow from ~4,600 today to ~47,000 (15,000 homes)【10†L125-L133】.  This suggests that capital-scarcity premiums in fringes have often been limited by large greenfield expansions.  

- **Context (Macro vs Vic tax):** It’s important not to blame the suburbs alone.  Victoria’s investor taxes were tightened post-2022 (land tax threshold cut to $50k【41†L143-L152】, $3.75k p.a. COVID levy on large landholdings【45†L76-L81】, foreign buyer surcharges up to 8%) which have hit investor returns and demand.  These policies likely depressed Victorian growth.  Across Australia, outer areas face similar yield constraints, but in Victoria the net after-tax return is lower.  For instance, Darwin now outperforms with yields ~6%【57†L151-L159】, and Queensland’s zero stamp duty regime has kept migration high.

In summary, **historical data do not strongly favor fringe Victorian suburbs** over 10 years.  Some growth has occurred, but often just matched the drag of financing costs.  In many cases, investors would have been better off in inner suburbs or diversified markets. 

## Supply-side analysis  

Melbourne’s geography allows multiple growth corridors, which tends to soften scarcity.  Beveridge (north) is one of several outer precincts (north-west, west, south-east) all adding large tracts of land.  For example, the Beveridge North West PSP itself unlocks 15,000 homes【10†L125-L133】; nearby areas like Craigieburn/Doreen/Clyde are adding tens of thousands more.  Tarneit grew by ~15,000 dwellings (2017–27 forecast)【25†L228-L236】.  By contrast, Sydney’s metropolitan fringe is constrained by the Blue Mountains and national parks, so even growing areas like Marsden Park or Oran Park face harder limits.  In Brisbane, land is relatively more abundant than Sydney (though still finite).  **Result:** Melbourne fringe suburb prices may only need modest growth to clear lots given ample supply, making land prices less sensitive to demand surges.

Victoria’s new taxes also act like “supply” by discouraging investors.  Land tax on investment properties jumped in 2024 (threshold to $50k【41†L143-L152】, plus sliding rates) and a “COVID Debt Levy” was added in 2023 to raise ~$1.2b/yr【45†L76-L81】.  These raise the ongoing cost of holding property in Victoria.  The consequence is falling investor demand (witness very low auction clearance rates in some corridors【64†L281-L290】) and, indirectly, higher rents as owners pass costs to tenants【41†L178-L183】.

Finally, Melbourne’s planning regime has aimed to supply more housing to curb prices.  The cumulative effect is that **outer suburbs face a continual pipeline of new competition**.  Unless Beveridge can establish genuine scarcity (e.g. unique amenity, or restrictions on new releases), it may struggle to outperform.

## Interstate fringe comparison  

Looking beyond Victoria, we see both similarities and differences:

- **Sydney (NSW):**  Fringe areas like Box Hill (NSW) show that prices can surge even with low yields.  Box Hill’s median house is ~$1.31m (April 2025) up 9.2% year-on-year【48†L30-L39】, with gross yield ~3.3%【48†L52-L56】 – similar yield to Beveridge but with far higher prices.  This is driven by Sydney’s limited land supply and strong immigration.  NSW has also eliminated stamp duty on principal residences, encouraging mobility, but still levies heavy taxes on investors.  In short, Sydney fringe could match or exceed Melbourne’s growth despite equally poor yields, largely because demand outstrips supply.

- **Brisbane/SEQ (Qld):**  Growth corridors like Springfield, Ripley and Yarrabilba have been high-growth.  Domain reports ~A$964k median for a 4-bed in Springfield (Mar 2025)【50†L79-L87】; typical rents ~$800pw (≈4.3% yield).  Queensland levies no land tax on residences and has generous First Home and stamp duty concessions, boosting migration.  Thus Brisbane fringes have delivered both decent growth and higher yields (often ~4–5%).  For example, Queensland’s vacancy rates were among the tightest nationally in early 2026, supporting rent rises.

- **Perth/Adelaide:** Perth’s oversupply pullback has driven yields to ~5–6%, but prices remain weak.  Adelaide fringes also offer ~5% yields.  These markets are smaller, with local jobs markets and immigration patterns quite different from Melbourne’s.  Investors seeking cashflow often shift here (e.g. Darwin, Perth).

- **Darwin/NT:**  Uniquely, Darwin now offers Australia’s highest gross yields (houses ~5.5%, units ~7.2%【57†L151-L159】) thanks to very tight vacancies (~0.5%【57†L111-L119】) and relatively low prices.  However, Darwin’s capital growth has been flat to volatile in the past.  

**Conclusion of comparison:**  Fringe suburbs everywhere tend to have yields in the 3–5% range.  Melbourne’s Beveridge looks riskier because of Victoria’s tax policies and massive future supply, not because the fringe concept is inherently doomed.  Sydney and Brisbane fringes can still outperform in boom times due to stronger immigration and constrained developable land (Sydney), or investor-friendly policy (Brisbane).  It’s likely a mix of both: Beveridge underperforms on a risk-adjusted basis partly because of **Vic-specific costs** and partly because Melbourne’s supply is very abundant.

## Established-suburb unit/townhouse alternative  

Buying closer to the city often means a smaller dwelling (unit or townhouse) within $600–750k.  These assets have very different characteristics:

- **Capital growth:**  Inner/suburbs saw slower growth recently.  For example, Hawthorn’s median house is ~$2.79m (down ~7% in 12m), median unit ~$570k (down ~1%)【54†L353-L359】.  Over 10 years, inner-city prices have been roughly flat in real terms (Melbourne median ~$980k now vs. ~$600k in 2012, ~4% p.a.), whereas established suburbs around $700k typically gave ~+30–40% in the last decade (3–4% p.a.).  Townhouses often appreciate closer to land values (maybe 5–6% p.a. long-term), but that value is mostly in their small land parcel. 

- **Rental yield:**  Units and townhouses generally offer higher gross yields.  In Hawthorn, houses yield 1.9% but units 5.0%【54†L353-L359】.  Other inner-ring areas show similar: e.g. Footscray/Brunswick units often 4.5–5%.  These yields easily match current mortgage rates, meaning far less negative gearing.  

- **Vacancy risk & demand:**  Units in high-demand areas (near transport, jobs) often have low vacancies (<2%), thanks to city renters and downsizers.  Many fringe areas face higher vacancy or turnover, especially as new stock comes online.  Established suburbs also benefit from strong owner-occupier demand, unlike far-out estates which are mostly investor-owned.

- **Costs:**  Units incur body-corporate fees (>$4,000/yr common) and land tax (if >$50k land value).  Houses avoid strata but pay full land tax on the block.  Overall, a smaller asset means less stamp duty, lower loans, and easier financing. 

- **Resale liquidity:**  Townhouses and units in popular suburbs often re-sell quickly (buyer pool includes families and upgraders).  By contrast, selling a new $700k house on the urban fringe can be slow, especially if many similar homes compete.

In short, an established-suburb unit/townhouse offers **similar or better yields** (reducing the cashflow drag) and similar long-run growth potential (land is scarce closer in).  The downside is less outright capital at stake and strata costs.  But from a risk-adjusted perspective, **the inner-suburb smaller dwelling looks like a less risky way to get ~$600–750k exposure than a large Beveridge house**.  (For example, a Hawthorn unit at $570k yields ~5.0%【54†L353-L359】 vs. Beveridge ~3.7%【5†L349-L352】, while still appreciating with Melbourne’s median.)

## Higher-yield property alternative  

Alternatively, one could chase **rental yield** elsewhere.  Properties with strong cashflow (and modest price) include smaller regional town houses or inner-city units.  Darwin and some Queensland/regional markets stand out:

- **Darwin:**  As noted, Darwin yields ~5.5% on houses and 7.2% on units【57†L151-L159】, by far the highest in Australia.  Vacancies there are ~0.5%【57†L111-L119】.  Investors have been flocking there despite minimal capital growth.  A $500k Darwin house yielding ~5.5% would cover 80% of a 6% mortgage in rent alone.

- **Other “high-yield” areas:**  Regional QLD (e.g. units in Brisbane outskirts) and some fringe Perth/Hobart properties often yield ~5%.  Even parts of Victoria like Ballarat or Bendigo can yield ~4–5%.  The trade-off is usually **lower expected capital appreciation**.  For example, Darwin prices have moved very little over the last decade despite yields, and these markets have smaller economic bases.

- **Cash-flow resilience:**  High-yield property would be mostly cashflow-positive or only mildly negative.  This greatly reduces risk in a rising-rate environment.  Holding costs can largely be met by rent, making negative gearing unnecessary.  

- **Downsides:**  Typically, these properties have *lower* capital growth potential.  Regional economies may stagnate, and resale prices may lag major cities.  But for an investor prioritising stable cashflow over land-gain, this may be preferable.  

In summary, if your main goal is reducing negative cashflow drag, buying in a high-yield market (Darwin/regional) is a better hedge against rising rates than betting on far-off land scarcity.  The investor would trade some long-run upside for immediate yield and lower holding costs.  

## ETF alternative  

A simple benchmark is an unleveraged equity portfolio, e.g. investing in a total stock market ETF.  Key points:

- **Long-term returns:**  Over the last 30 years, Australian equities (ASX All Ords) returned ~10.8% p.a. including dividends【59†L138-L146】; global equities ~8–9%.  This dwarfs typical property returns.  More conservatively, post-2010 equities have still returned ~5–7% p.a. with large volatility swings.  In recent years (post-2010), annual returns have been around 5–7% on average, depending on starting point. (By comparison, Melbourne house values were largely flat or down from 2012–2022.)  

- **Dividend yield:**  The ASX200 currently yields ~4% through dividends (mostly franked).  This is on par with high-end property yields.  Unlike property, dividends (and any capital gains) can be tax-advantaged via franking credits and the CGT discount for long-term holdings.

- **Costs:**  ETFs have very low fees (<0.1% p.a.) and no transaction costs aside from brokerage (which is small on large investments).  Crucially, there is no stamp duty, land tax, or maintenance costs.  Liquidity is instant – you can adjust your portfolio daily.

- **Diversification:**  A broad ETF spreads risk over 200+ companies and industries.  By contrast, a single property is concentrated in one location and sector.  Equity portfolios also capture global growth, not just domestic population growth.

- **Leverage:**  While one *can* borrow to buy ETFs (margin loans), the typical investor will hold them unleveraged.  In property, leverage 4–7× amplifies both returns and losses.  On a risk-adjusted basis, a 4% yield on a $750k portfolio funded 100% with cash is less risky than a leveraged property at 4% yield.

- **Behaviour:**  Equities require less “hands-on” (no tenants, no repairs), but are subject to market swings.  Property is more stable month-to-month but has lumpy illiquidity.

Overall, **after costs and risk-adjustment, broad ETFs often match or beat property returns**.  For example, $100k invested in the ASX300 (with dividends reinvested) in 1995 would be ~$640k today【59†L138-L146】, whereas the same capital in property would likely yield much less (remember that an unleveraged $100k property deposit buys ~$500k house; 30 years of property had far lower compounding).  Thus, for an investor not needing leverage, ETFs provide a strong, simple alternative, especially given today’s low yields and high financing costs in property.

## Scenarios  

**Bull case (upside):**  Infrastructure delivers on schedule: a new Beveridge station, upgraded rail/roads, the intermodal freight hub. Regional and overseas migration continues strongly into northern Melbourne.  In this case, demand could outstrip supply in the long run.  Bev­eridge might then see ~5–6% p.a. capital growth over decades (similar to optimistic growth corridors in history).  Rents could rise above current forecasts if shortages emerge.  In such a scenario, a negative-cashflow phase (perhaps 5–10 years) would be “paid back” by outsized growth in later years.  Beveridge land + infrastructure could eventually outperform smaller dwellings.

**Base case (moderate growth):**  Infrastructure comes more slowly.  Melbourne population grows at a normal (~1–2%) pace, but new Beveridge developments keep prices in check.  Beveridge might achieve ~3% p.a. appreciation – roughly in line with historical trends pre-tax.  Negative gearing lasts ~10 years before cash flows approach break-even.  Over 30 years, total return might be ~6–7% p.a. (including 3% rents and 3% growth) before costs, which is similar to or slightly below an ETF after leverage and tax.  Here, holders must endure prolonged losses initially and will only break even or slightly win on total return compared to a leveraged ETF or inner-suburb.

**Bear case (downside):**  Interest rates stay high (5–7%), Victoria’s economy slows, and Beveridge supply overwhelms demand.  New stock continues to flood the market (e.g. PSP uptake exceeds estimates), and planned infrastructure is delayed.  Rental vacancies rise (e.g. to 3–5%) and yields fall further.  Price growth stalls or even goes negative for a decade.  The investor continues losing >$5k/yr after tax for 10+ years.  Only much later (e.g. after 15–20 years) might compounding of moderate growth recoup losses.  Under this scenario, Beveridge would be a poor outcome compared to most alternatives.

These scenarios show Beveridge is **highly dependent on optimistic assumptions** (timely infrastructure, strong demand).  Any slip into the bear/base realm yields outcomes worse than simpler strategies.

## Conclusion  

Buying in Beveridge means betting on *future* demand and scarcity.  The **base-case outlook is underwhelming**: very low initial yields (3–4%) at high financing costs, heavy ongoing taxes, and enormous future supply suggest years of negative cashflow.  Historical fringe growth in Melbourne *did not easily recoup* such cash deficits, except during ultra-low-rate booms.  By contrast, an inner-city unit/townhouse or even a leveraged ETF portfolio would have delivered similar or better returns with far less risk and effort.  

For Beveridge to outperform over 30 years, a few conditions must hold: Melbourne must grow much faster than expected, with millions of new residents pouring into the north; infrastructure (train, roads, freight) must be delivered on time; and no other growth corridor siphons off demand.  Only then would the large land banks in Beveridge become scarce and expensive enough to reward early investors.  If any of these fail (e.g. migration slows, tax policy remains high, supply outpaces demand), Beveridge’s extra risks will outweigh its upside.  In that light, **a diversified ETF or higher-yield property might be a more rational path** for an investor seeking solid long-term returns with less cashflow pain.
